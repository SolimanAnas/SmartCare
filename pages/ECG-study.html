The electrical conduction system

Where every heartbeat begins — and why the ECG looks the way it does

The heart is its own power plant. A wave of electricity is born in the sinoatrial (SA) node, spreads through the atria, pauses at the gatekeeper AV node, then races down the His–Purkinje highway to fire the ventricles. The ECG is simply that electrical journey, recorded from the skin. Remember: the ECG shows electrical activity only — it tells you nothing about whether the heart is actually pumping. Always treat the patient, not the monitor.

The pacemaker hierarchy. If the SA node fails, the next site down takes over — but slower. This is the escape mechanism behind junctional (40–60) and idioventricular (20–40) rhythms.
◆ The pacemaker ladder

Each level can pace the heart if the one above it fails — SA (60–100) → AV junction (40–60) → ventricle/Purkinje (20–40). The faster site normally wins and "overdrive-suppresses" the rest.

♥ Why the AV delay matters

The AV node deliberately slows the impulse ~0.1 s so the atria finish emptying ("atrial kick") before the ventricles contract. That pause is your PR interval, and it's also the heart's overload protection in atrial flutter/fib.

⚠ The single most useful question

Before naming any rhythm, ask: "Is the QRS narrow or wide?" Narrow (<0.12 s) means the impulse used the normal His–Purkinje highway (atrial/junctional origin). Wide means it either started in the ventricle or took a detour (bundle branch block, pacing, pre-excitation).

QT interval — the danger interval

✕ Prolonged QT

Caused by drugs (amiodarone, droperidol, haloperidol, erythromycin, methadone, procainamide, tricyclics) or conditions (CHF, MI, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypocalcemia, myocarditis). A long QT is the launch pad for Torsades de Pointes.

♥ Shortened QT

Caused by digoxin, phenothiazines, or hypercalcemia / hyperkalemia.

ECG paper & calculating rate

Time runs left-to-right, voltage runs up-and-down

ECG paper moves at a constant 25 mm/sec. That fixed speed turns distance into time, and lets you measure rate, intervals, and regularity with nothing but your eyes.

The grid. Horizontal = time, vertical = voltage.

Time & voltage cheat-sheet

Small box0.04 s1 mm / 0.1 mV
Large box0.20 s5 mm / 0.5 mV
5 large boxes1.00 s25 mm
Paper speed25 mm/sstandard

Three ways to calculate heart rate

① The 300 method

Count large boxes between two R waves and divide into 300.

300 ÷ big boxes = bpm

Sequence to memorize: 300 · 150 · 100 · 75 · 60 · 50. Best for regular rhythms.

② The 1500 method

Count small boxes between two R waves and divide into 1500.

1500 ÷ small boxes = bpm

Most precise. Use for regular rhythms when you need an exact number.

③ The 6-second method

Count QRS complexes in a 6-second strip and multiply by 10.

QRS in 6 s × 10 = bpm

The only reliable method for irregular rhythms (A-fib, MAT).

♥ When atrial ≠ ventricular

In blocks (especially 3°) the atria and ventricles march at different rates. Measure both: P-to-P for the atrial rate, R-to-R for the ventricular rate.

The 12 leads — 12 camera angles

Each lead is a different view of the same electrical event

A 12-lead ECG is one heart photographed from twelve directions. Ten electrodes (four limb + six chest) generate twelve "views." Six look at the heart's frontal plane (limb leads); six look at the horizontal plane (chest leads). Group them by the heart wall they watch, and the ECG starts telling you where a problem is.

Lead groupLeadsHeart wallCoronary artery
InferiorII, III, aVFBottom of heartRight coronary (RCA)
SeptalV1, V2Interventricular septumLAD
AnteriorV3, V4Front wallLAD
LateralI, aVL, V5, V6Left side wallCircumflex
♥ Reciprocal "mirror" leads

Leads opposite the injured wall show the mirror image — ST depression opposite ST elevation. Spotting reciprocal change confirms a true STEMI.

⚠ aVR — the lonely lead

Lead aVR often shows no change in an MI. ST elevation in aVR (with diffuse depression elsewhere) suggests left main or severe triple-vessel disease.

◆ Combined territory

An MI rarely respects a single wall. ST elevation in V3–V4 (anterior) plus I, aVL, V5, V6 (lateral) = an anterolateral infarction. Always name what you see in every group.

◆ Grouping ectopy

Bigeminy = every other beat is premature. Trigeminy = every third. Couplet = 2 in a row. Triplet/run = 3+ (a salvo of VT).

♥ Pause type after a PVC

Compensatory (complete) pause → SA node timing untouched (classic PVC). Non-compensatory (incomplete) → the ectopic reset the SA node (classic PAC).

Ischemia, injury & infarction

Reading the 12-lead for a heart attack

When a coronary artery clogs, the muscle it feeds passes through three stages, each with its own ECG fingerprint. Catching them early — and locating them by lead — is the whole game in prehospital and ED cardiology.

① Ischemia

Oxygen-starved but alive. ST depression and/or T-wave inversion. Reversible if flow is restored.

② Injury

Acute, ongoing occlusion. ST elevation — the hallmark of a STEMI. Time-critical: muscle is dying now.

③ Infarction

Dead tissue. Pathologic Q waves. After the event, ST returns to baseline & T normalizes, but the Q wave is a permanent scar.

The ST segment relative to baseline is the single most important finding in chest-pain triage.
✕ STEMI criteria

ST elevation >1 mm in limb leads or >2 mm in chest leads, in ≥2 contiguous leads, signals an evolving MI from complete epicardial coronary occlusion. This is a cath-lab activation. Other causes: pericarditis, ventricular aneurysm, early repolarization.

⚠ NSTEMI

Partial occlusion → ST depression and/or T inversion without ST elevation = NSTEMI. Also caused by LVH, conduction defects, and digoxin effect — correlate clinically.

EMS 12-leads with transmitted STEMI alerts shorten door-to-balloon time. If inferior MI is present, capture a right-sided (V4R) and posterior (V7–V9) ECG before giving nitroglycerin.

ACLS rhythm algorithms

From recognition to the right intervention

Recognition is only half the job — these four algorithms turn a rhythm into an action. They follow the structure of the ACLS Rhythms for the ACLS Algorithms guide.

① Cardiac arrest — the H's & T's

For VF / pulseless VT: high-quality CPR + early defibrillation is everything. For PEA and asystole: CPR + epinephrine + relentlessly hunt the reversible cause. The longer the delay, the lower the chance of conversion.

The 5 H's

    • Hypovolemia
    • Hypoxia
    • Hydrogen ion (acidosis)
    • Hypo / Hyperkalemia
    • Hypothermia

The 5 T's

  • Tablets / toxins (drug OD)
  • Tamponade (cardiac)
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Thrombosis — coronary (ACS)
  • Thrombosis — pulmonary (PE)

✕ Asystole confirmation

Before calling asystole, rule out fine VF and the mimics: loose leads, no power, low gain. "Flatline" in two leads, with a treatable cause excluded, and DNAR status checked.

⚠ Wide & irregular = danger

Treat wide-complex irregular tachycardia as polymorphic VT or pre-excited A-fib (WPW) until proven otherwise. In WPW + A-fib, AV-nodal blockers (adenosine, β-blockers, Ca²⁺ blockers, digoxin) are class III — can be lethal.

⚠ Atropine won't fix infranodal block

Atropine is often ineffective for Mobitz II and complete heart block (the block is below the AV node). Go straight to pacing. New-onset Type II or 3° block with an ACS is an indication for a transvenous pacemaker. Never treat 3° block + ventricular escape beats with lidocaine — you may abolish the only rhythm keeping the patient alive.

④ Transcutaneous pacing (TCP)

StepWhat you see
Pads on, pacer offUnderlying bradycardia (e.g., 3° block at 41/min, wide escape).
Below thresholdPacing spike marker present but no capture — spike not followed by a wide QRS.
Above thresholdElectrical capture: each spike → a broad QRS with a T wave of opposite polarity (looks PVC-like). Then confirm mechanical capture with a palpable pulse.
♥ Capture, both kinds

Electrical capture = spike makes a QRS on the monitor. Mechanical capture = that QRS makes a pulse you can feel. You need both. Use sedation/analgesia — TCP hurts.
